Solving the Cubic Equation for Dummies

Originally published October 1, 2016
Physics Forums / stevendaryl

Everybody learns the “quadratic formula” for solving equations of the form Ax2+Bx+C=0Ax2+Bx+C=0, even though you don’t really need such a formula, because you can solve for xx through the technique of “completing the square”. What you need a formula for is the solution to the cubic equation: Ax3+Bx2+Cx+D=0Ax3+Bx2+Cx+D=0. There is no obvious way that “completing the cube” makes the solution into a matter of just taking cube roots in the same way that “completing the square” solves the quadratic in terms of square roots. So how could we derive such a cubic formula? Let’s see if I can convince you that you could derive it, if you thought about it hard enough, in the right way.

We’re given Ax3+Bx2+Cx+D=0Ax3+Bx2+Cx+D=0. Let’s assume that A=1A=1, so we’re interested in solving an equation of the form x3+Bx2+Cx+D=0x3+Bx2+Cx+D=0. (A moment’s thought will show you that if you can solve that case, you can solve the general case). So by finding a “cubic formula”, we mean finding a function f(B,C,D)f(B,C,D) that gives the solution. However, in general, a cubic equation has three solutions. So such a function must be multi-valued, with three possible values.

In the quadratic case, the solution is x=−b±b2–4ac√2ax=−b±b2–4ac2a. We can rewrite this in the following form:
x=−b2a+rb2–4ac√2ax=−b2a+rb2–4ac2a, where rr is either plus or minus 1, that is, one of the two solutions of r2=1r2=1.

Generalizing from the quadratic case, let’s assume that the multi-valuedness comes from the three branches of the cube root function. In other words, let’s look for a function of the form:

f(B,C,D,r)f(B,C,D,r)

such that you get a solution whenever the parameter rr is one of the cube-roots of 1. Let’s fix B,C,DB,C,D and consider ff as a function of rr. The simplest possible non-constant functions are polynomials. So let’s assume that ff has the form: f(r)=α+βr+γr2f(r)=α+βr+γr2 (where α,β,γα,β,γ are implicitly dependent on the coefficients B,C,DB,C,D of the original cubic equaion.) Since we ultimately want rr to be a cube-root of 1, we don’t need to consider terms of the form r3r3 or higher-order, because those terms are reducible to lower-order terms if r3=1r3=1

A few facts about the cube roots of 1. There are three cube roots, and they can be written in the form e2nπi3e2nπi3. Concretely,

r1=−12+3√2ir1=−12+32i

r2=−12–3√2ir2=−12–32i

r3=1r3=1 corresponding to n=1,2,3n=1,2,3

We can see that r2r2 is just the square of r1r1, so we can just write the three solutions as:

r1=qr1=q

r2=q2r2=q2

r3=1r3=1

where qq is e2πi3e2πi3. Another interesting fact about the solutions is that

r1+r2+r3=0r1+r2+r3=0

 

a fact that can be proved by substitution, or geometrically, by representing the cube roots of 1 as vectors in 2-D complex space.

So we know that 1+q+q2=01+q+q2=0 where qq is as defined.

So in terms of our function f(r)f(r), we want the three solutions to our cubic equation to be: f(q),f(q2),f(1)f(q),f(q2),f(1). Using our polynomial for ff, this means that we want the three solutions to be:

x1=α+βq+γq2x1=α+βq+γq2

x2=α+βq2+γq4=α+βq2+γqx2=α+βq2+γq4=α+βq2+γq

Let’s work out a few symmetric combinations of these solutions that will be needed:

x1+x2+x3=3αx1+x2+x3=3α (All other terms vanish, when we use the fact that 1+q+q2=0)1+q+q2=0)
x1x2+x1x3+x2x3=3(α2–βγ)x1x2+x1x3+x2x3=3(α2–βγ) (Again, we used 1+q+q2=01+q+q2=0 to get this).

The significance of these combinations is that zeros of a polynomial can be used to factor the polynomial: If x1,x2,x3x1,x2,x3 are the three solutions of x3+Bx2+Cx+D=0x3+Bx2+Cx+D=0, then we can write:

x3+Bx2+Cx+D=(x−x1)(x−x2)(x−x3)=x3–(x1+x2+x3)x2+(x1x2+x1x3+x2x3)x–(x1x2x3)x3+Bx2+Cx+D=(x−x1)(x−x2)(x−x3)=x3–(x1+x2+x3)x2+(x1x2+x1x3+x2x3)x–(x1x2x3)

Comparing coefficients tells us that

(x1+x2+x3)=−B(x1+x2+x3)=−B
(x1x2+x1x3+x2x3)=C(x1x2+x1x3+x2x3)=C
(x1x2x3)=−D(x1x2x3)=−D

So using our previous results for those combinations of solutions, we find:

3α=−B3α=−B. So α=−B3α=−B3
3(α2–βγ)=C3(α2–βγ)=C. So γ=B29–C3βγ=B29–C3β
α3+β3+γ3–3αβγ=−Dα3+β3+γ3–3αβγ=−D.

In the last equation, we can substitute for αα and γγ to get:

−B327+D+B(B29–C3)+β3+(B29–C3)3β3=0−B327+D+B(B29–C3)+β3+(B29–C3)3β3=0

We can multiply by β3β3 to get:

β6+(−B327+D+B(B29–C3))β3+(B29–C3)3)=0β6+(−B327+D+B(B29–C3))β3+(B29–C3)3)=0

Oh no! After all that work to find the solution to a 3rd degree equation, we end up with a 6th degree equation for the coefficient ββ. Is that progress? Well, yes it is. Because it’s only a quadratic equation in β3β3:

(β3)2+(−B327+D+B(B29–C3))β3+(B29–C3)3)=0(β3)2+(−B327+D+B(B29–C3))β3+(B29–C3)3)=0

So we can solve this equation for β3β3, take the cube root of the result to get ββ (any cube root will do), and then use the previously derived relationship between ββ and γγ to find γγ. We already solved for αα. So then we know all three coefficients in our function.

Then in terms of these values for α,β,γα,β,γ, we can find our three solutions to the original cubic equation:
x1=α+βq+γq2x1=α+βq+γq2
x2=α+βq2+γqx2=α+βq2+γq
x3=α+β+γx3=α+β+γ
(where, remember, q is one of the complex cube roots of 1)

I’m not going to go to the trouble of writing out the explicit solutions in terms of the coefficients of the original cubic equation. The point here is to convince you that you could compute them yourself, if forced to at gunpoint.

Obviously, this general strategy for solving a polynomial equation generalizes to any degree. However, the last stroke of luck, that the 6th degree polynomial equation for one of the coefficients can be rewritten as a quadratic equation, was unexpected. There is no reason to think that such luck will hold out for higher-degree polynomials. As a matter of fact, the French mathematician Evariste Galois, who tragically died in a duel, proved that techniques along the lines of those used here can’t be used to solve a general 5th order (or higher order) equation.
